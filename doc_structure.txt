[Heading 1] ABSTRACT
[Normal] Cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) remain one of the leading causes of death worldwide, with arrhythmias being a significant contributing factor. Accurate and efficient detection of arrhythmias is crucial for timely diagnosis and treatment. The electrocardiogram (ECG) is a vital diagnostic tool for monitoring heart conditions, but manual ECG interpretation can be labour-intensive and prone to errors.
[Normal] To address this, we initially employed various machine learning models to classify ECG signals, assessing their performance based on accuracy. Based on the highest-performing model, we then developed ensemble models, combining it with other classifiers to further enhance performance. While ensemble methods improved classification accuracy, the need for better generalization and feature extraction motivated the development of a more advanced deep learning approach.
[Normal] This project proposes a cross-optimized hybrid deep learning framework for ECG signal classification that moves beyond the conventional serial hybrid design. Instead of feeding final outputs from one model into another, the proposed method enables mutual parameter exchange, where intermediate features extracted by Model A are shared with Model B, and vice versa, allowing joint optimization.
[Normal] By leveraging both temporal (time-domain morphology) and spectral (frequency-domain dynamics) representations of ECG signals, this framework aims to capture a richer set of discriminative features while minimizing overfitting. The system will be implemented on publicly available ECG datasets in Google Co-lab, validated against standard arrhythmia classification benchmarks, and evaluated using accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, and F1-score.

[Normal] 1.1 Introduction to Heart Diseases
[Normal] Heart disease, also known as cardiovascular disease, refers to a group of conditions that affect the structure and function of the heart and blood vessels. It encompasses a wide range of disorders, including coronary artery disease, heart failure, arrhythmias, valvular heart disease, and congenital heart defects, among others. Heart disease is a leading cause of morbidity and mortality globally, posing a significant public health challenge.
[Normal] Cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) are a group of disorders that affect the heart and blood vessels, often leading to serious complications such as heart attacks, strokes, and peripheral artery disease. These conditions are among the leading causes of mortality worldwide, posing a significant public health challenge that encompass a range of disorders, including coronary artery disease, hypertension (high blood pressure), heart failure, arrhythmia (irregular heartbeats), and peripheral vascular disease. They can develop due to various factors, including genetics, lifestyle choices, and underlying medical conditions. One of the primary contributors to CVDs is atherosclerosis, a condition characterized by the buildup of plague within the arteries, restricts blood flow, and can eventually lead to complications such as heart attacks and strokes.
[Normal] Risk factors for cardiovascular diseases include smoking, poor diet, lack of physical activity, obesity, high blood pressure, diabetes, and high cholesterol levels. Additionally, factors such as age, gender, and family history can influence an individual's susceptibility to developing CVDs.The prevention and management of cardiovascular diseases often involve lifestyle modifications such as maintaining a healthy diet, regular exercise, avoiding tobacco use, managing stress, and controlling underlying medical conditions. Medications may also be prescribed to control risk factors such as hypertension, high cholesterol, and diabetes.
[Normal] Early detection and timely intervention are crucial in mitigating the impact of CVDs. Regular screenings, such as blood pressure checks and cholesterol tests, can help identify risk factors and allow for appropriate management strategies to be implemented. Overall, addressing cardiovascular diseases requires a comprehensive approach that involves both individual lifestyle changes and broader public health initiatives aimed at raising awareness, promoting healthy behaviors, and improving access to healthcare services. By prioritizing prevention and early intervention, the burden of CVDs can be significantly reduced, leading to improved health outcomes and quality of life for individuals worldwide.
[Normal] Types of Heart diseases:
[Caption] Fig 1.1 Types of heart diseases
[Normal] Fig 1.1 shows various types of heart diseases, coronary artery disease (CAD): CAD is the most common type of heart disease and occurs when the coronary arteries, which supply oxygen-rich blood to the heart muscle, become narrowed or blocked by a buildup of plaque (atherosclerosis). This restricts blood flow to the heart, leading to chest pain (angina), heart attacks (myocardial infarctions), and potentially fatal complications. It is the damage or disease in the heart’s major blood vessels. The usual cause is the buildup of plaque. This causes coronary arteries to narrow, limiting blood flow to the heart. Stroke is the Damage to the brain from interruption of its blood supply.
[Normal] Heart Failure: Heart failure occurs when the heart is unable to pump blood effectively to meet the body's demands. It can result from various underlying conditions, such as CAD, hypertension, diabetes, or damage to the heart muscle (cardiomyopathy). Symptoms of heart failure include shortness of breath, fatigue, swelling of the legs and ankles, and difficulty performing daily activities.
[Normal] Arrhythmias: Arrhythmias are abnormal heart rhythms that occur when the electrical impulses that coordinate heartbeats are disrupted. This can cause the heart to beat too fast (tachycardia), too slow (bradycardia), or irregularly. Arrhythmias can lead to palpitations, dizziness, fainting, and in severe cases, cardiac arrest.
[Normal] Valvular Heart Disease: Valvular heart disease involves abnormalities or damage to the heart valves, which regulate blood flow within the heart chambers. This can result in valve stenosis (narrowing) or regurgitation (leakage), impairing the heart's ability to pump blood efficiently and causing symptoms such as chest pain, fatigue, and shortness of breath.
[Normal] Congenital Heart Defects: Congenital heart defects are structural abnormalities of the heart that are present at birth. These defects can affect the heart chambers, valves, or major blood vessels, leading to disturbances in blood flow and oxygen delivery. Depending on the severity and type of defect, individuals may experience a range of symptoms from mild to life-threatening.
[Normal] Importance of early detection and diagnosis of heart disease
[Normal] Heart disease remains one of the leading causes of morbidity and mortality worldwide, imposing a significant burden on healthcare systems and individuals alike. With its diverse manifestations and potentially fatal consequences, the importance of early detection and diagnosis of heart disease cannot be overstated. Timely identification of cardiac abnormalities enables healthcare providers to initiate appropriate interventions, mitigate risks, and improve patient outcomes.
[Normal] Early detection plays a pivotal role in preventing the progression of heart disease and reducing associated complications.
[Normal] Many cardiovascular conditions, including coronary artery disease, arrhythmia, and heart failure, often develop gradually over time, with subtle or asymptomatic presentations in the early stages. As a result, individuals may remain unaware of their underlying cardiac issues until they experience severe symptoms or adverse events. By implementing screening programs and diagnostic protocols that target high-risk populations or individuals with predisposing factors, healthcare providers can identify cardiac abnormalities at an early stage, facilitating prompt intervention and management.
[Normal] Furthermore, early diagnosis of heart disease offers opportunities for preventive strategies and lifestyle modifications that can mitigate risk factors and improve cardiovascular health. Lifestyle factors such as diet, physical activity, and smoking habits significantly influence the development and progression of heart disease. By identifying individuals at risk and providing education, counseling, and support for adopting healthier behaviors, healthcare providers can empower patients to take proactive steps towards reducing their cardiovascular risk and optimizing their overall well-being.
[Normal] In addition to its clinical implications, early detection of heart disease carries significant socioeconomic benefits. The economic burden associated with managing advanced stages of heart disease, including hospitalizations, surgeries, and long-term care, can be substantial. By detecting cardiac abnormalities early in the disease course and implementing preventive measures, healthcare systems can reduce healthcare expenditures, improve resource allocation, and enhance the quality-of-care delivery.
[Normal] The importance of early detection and diagnosis of heart disease lies not only in its potential to improve individual health outcomes but also in its broader impact on healthcare systems and society. Through proactive screening, timely intervention, and preventive strategies, we can strive towards reducing the global burden of heart disease and promoting cardiovascular health for all.
[Normal] 1.2 Electrocardiogram
[Normal] An electrocardiogram (ECG or EKG) is a diagnostic test that records the electrical activity of the heart over a period of time. This non-invasive procedure is commonly used to evaluate the heart's rhythm and electrical conduction, helping healthcare providers assess cardiac health and detect various abnormalities.
[Normal] During an ECG, electrodes are placed on specific locations on the skin, typically on the chest, arms, and legs. These electrodes detect the electrical signals generated by the heart as it contracts and relaxes. The signals are then amplified and recorded by the ECG machine, producing a visual representation of the heart's electrical activity known as an electrocardiogram. The ECG waveform consists of several components, including the P wave, QRS complex, and T wave. The Fig 1.2 shows ECG components P wave, QRS complex and T wave. Each component corresponds to a specific phase of the cardiac cycle: P wave: Represents the depolarization (contraction) of the atria, or upper chambers of the heart. QRS complex: Reflects the depolarization of the ventricles, or lower chambers of the heart, as well as the contraction of the ventricles.
[Normal] T wave: Indicates the repolarization (relaxation) of the ventricles, preparing them for the next heartbeat.
[Normal] Arrhythmias: Irregular heart rhythms, such as atrial fibrillation and ventricular tachycardia.
[Normal] Myocardial infarction (heart attack): Characterized by specific changes in the ECG pattern, such as ST-segment elevation or Q-wave formation.
[Normal] Cardiac ischemia: Reduced blood flow to the heart muscle, often indicated by ST-segment depression or T-wave inversion.
[Normal] Cardiac hypertrophy: Enlargement of the heart chambers, resulting in changes to the ECG waveform.ECGs are valuable tools in diagnosing and monitoring various cardiovascular conditions. They are routinely used in clinical settings, including hospitals, clinics, and emergency departments. ECGs can provide valuable information quickly and efficiently, aiding healthcare providers in making timely and accurate decisions regarding patient care and treatment.

[Normal] Beat Classification:
[Normal] Beat classification, also known as heartbeat classification or cardiac rhythm classification, is a process of categorizing individual heartbeats into different classes based on their characteristics. This classification is essential in analyzing electrocardiogram (ECG) signals, which record the electrical activity of the heart over time. Beat classification plays a crucial role in various medical applications, including the detection and diagnosis of cardiac arrhythmias, monitoring patient health, and guiding treatment decisions.
[Normal] Beat classification involves several steps:
[Normal] Signal Preprocessing: ECG signals are preprocessed to remove noise, baseline wander, and artifacts that may interfere with accurate beat detection and classification.
[Normal] Feature Extraction: Relevant features are extracted from each heartbeat, such as amplitude, duration, morphology, and intervals (e.g., P-QRS-T intervals).
[Normal] Classification: Machine learning algorithms or pattern recognition techniques are applied to classify heartbeats into different categories based on extracted features. Common classification approaches include rule-based algorithms, template matching, support vector machines (SVM), artificial neural networks (ANN), and convolutional neural networks (CNN).
[Normal] Beat Types:
[Normal] Normal Beats: Normal beats, also known as sinus beats, represent the typical rhythmic contractions of the heart generated by the sinoatrial (SA) node. These beats exhibit regular morphology and timing, characterized by a consistent sequence of P waves, QRS complexes, and T waves on the electrocardiogram (ECG). Normal beats indicate a healthy sinus rhythm and are essential for maintaining efficient cardiac function.
[Normal] Premature Beats: Premature beats, also called ectopic beats, occur earlier than expected in the cardiac cycle and originate from ectopic foci outside the SA node. Premature beats can manifest as premature atrial contractions (PACs) or premature ventricular contractions (PVCs), depending on their site of origin. PACs arise from abnormal electrical impulses originating in the atria, resulting in premature atrial depolarization and distinctive premature P waves on the ECG. PVCs originate from the ventricles and produce premature, wide QRS complexes on the ECG. While occasional premature beats are common and may not indicate underlying pathology, frequent or sustained ectopic beats can be symptomatic and may require further evaluation.
[Normal] Supraventricular Arrhythmias: Supraventricular arrhythmias encompass a group of abnormal heart rhythms originating above the ventricles, typically within the atria or atrioventricular (AV) node. These arrhythmias include:
[Normal] Atrial Fibrillation (AF): AF is the most common sustained cardiac arrhythmia, characterized by rapid, irregular electrical activity in the atria, leading to disorganized atrial contractions. AF may result in palpitations, dizziness, fatigue, and an increased risk of stroke and heart failure.
[Normal] Atrial Flutter: Atrial flutter is a rhythmic, sawtooth pattern of atrial depolarization, typically occurring at a faster rate than normal sinus rhythm. Atrial flutter may cause palpitations, shortness of breath, and hemodynamic compromise.
[Normal] Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT): SVT refers to a rapid heart rate originating above the ventricles, often arising from reentrant circuits within the atria or AV node. SVT can cause palpitations, chest discomfort, dizziness, and syncope.
[Normal] Ventricular Arrhythmias: Ventricular arrhythmias arise from abnormal electrical activity originating in the ventricles, leading to irregular heart rhythms and compromised cardiac function. These arrhythmias include:
[Normal] Ventricular Tachycardia (VT): VT is characterized by a rapid, regular or irregular heartbeat originating from the ventricles, typically with a wide QRS complex on the ECG. VT can be sustained or nonsustained and may result in hemodynamic instability and sudden cardiac arrest.
[Normal] Ventricular Fibrillation (VF): VF is a life-threatening arrhythmia characterized by rapid, chaotic electrical activity in the ventricles, leading to ineffective ventricular contractions and loss of cardiac output. VF results in immediate loss of consciousness and requires immediate defibrillation to restore normal heart rhythm and prevent sudden cardiac death.
[Normal] Ventricular Ectopic Beats: Ventricular ectopic beats, or premature ventricular contractions (PVCs), originate from ectopic foci within the ventricles, resulting in premature depolarization and distinctive wide QRS complexes on the ECG. Frequent PVCs may indicate underlying cardiac pathology and may warrant further evaluation.

[Normal] PAPER 1
[Normal] The study " Hybrid CNN–Transformer for Arrhythmia Kim et al., 2025, IEEE Transactions on Biomedical Engineering paper description develops a deep learning framework that combines Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) with Transformer architectures to classify cardiac arrhythmias from electrocardiogram (ECG) data. Unlike many classical models, this hybrid approach is designed to extract both localized signal features and long-range temporal dependencies in ECG recordings for more accurate arrhythmia detection.
[Normal] PAPER 2
[Normal] The study “Cardio Attention Net He et al., 2025, Nature Biomedical Engineering ,He and colleagues focused on interpretability. They introduced Cardio Attention Net with attention mechanisms that highlighted critical ECG segments during classification. This design not only improved performance but also provided transparency by showing clinicians exactly which waveform segments influenced the model's decision, making the system more trustworthy in clinical settings. It interprets the Attention mechanisms and highlight the critical ECG segments
[Normal] PAPER 3
[Normal] The paper titled “Hybrid CNN-GRU-Transformer by Dhara et al.,2025,Computers in Biology and Medicine” introduces The model processes ECG time-series to detect cardiovascular disease (CVD) and arrhythmia patterns with improved reliability by learning both spatial morphology and temporal rhythm changes. In experiments on the PTB-XL ECG database, the hybrid model achieved high classification performance — with reported accuracy around ~98.8%, low false-negative rates, and low false-positive rates, outperforming versions of the architecture with individual components removed (ablation study)).
[Normal] PAPER 4
[Normal] The study " Multi-Scale CNN–Transformer by Wei & Li, 2025, Artificial Intelligence in Medicine. Wei and Li developed a multi-scale CNN that processed ECGs at varying resolutions using kernels of different sizes. This allowed the model to detect both short, localized abnormalities and long-duration rhythm disorders. A Transformer then integrated these features into a unified sequence representation, yielding robust arrhythmia detection across multiple scales.
[Normal] PAPER 5
[Normal] The study “Adaptive Cross-Attention CNN–Transformer by Hu et al., 2025, Medical Image Analysis explains a cross-attention mechanism that emphasizes clinically important ECG segments while suppressing noise. CNN features are refined using Transformer-based temporal context. The adaptive attention enables accurate identification of abnormal beats, particularly in challenging cases such as atrial fibrillation, improving classification reliability.
[Normal] PAPER 6
[Normal] The study " CNN–Transformer + Genetic Algorithm by Krishna et al., 2024,Knowledge Based systems” this approach integrates a Genetic Algorithm with a CNN–Transformer model to automate architecture optimization. The GA evolves hyperparameters and structural configurations iteratively, reducing manual tuning effort. The result is a computationally efficient yet powerful arrhythmia detection system suitable for practical deployment.
[Normal] PAPER 7
[Normal] The study “ Explainable Multimodal Fusion by Oladunni & Aneni, 2025, Journal of Biomedical Informatics” this paper presents a multimodal fusion framework combining ECG, PPG, and clinical metadata using attention-based integration. The model identifies which modality contributes most to each prediction, enhancing interpretability. [Normal] 4.1 Introduction to Deep Learning
[Normal] Deep learning is a subset of machine learning that focuses on learning representations of data through hierarchical layers of artificial neural networks. Unlike traditional machine learning algorithms that require handcrafted feature extraction, deep learning algorithms can automatically learn feature representations from raw data. Deep learning models, known as deep neural networks, consist of multiple layers of interconnected neurons that process data in a hierarchical fashion, with each layer learning increasingly abstract features.
[Normal] The term "deep" in deep learning refers to the depth of these neural networks, which can have many layers, typically ranging from tens to hundreds or even thousands of layers. Each layer in a deep neural network performs a transformation on the input data, and the output of one layer serves as the input to the next layer. The final layer of the network produces the desired output, such as a classification label or a regression value.
[Normal] Deep learning has achieved remarkable success in various domains, including computer vision, natural language processing, speech recognition, and healthcare. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are commonly used for image analysis tasks such as image classification, object detection, and image segmentation. Recurrent neural networks (RNNs) are well-suited for sequential data processing tasks such as language translation, speech recognition, and time series prediction.
[Normal] One of the key advantages of deep learning is its ability to automatically learn hierarchical representations of data, capturing intricate patterns and relationships that may be difficult to specify manually. Deep learning models can handle large volumes of data and learn complex functions, making them particularly effective for tasks with high-dimensional inputs and outputs.
[Normal] Deep learning continues to advance rapidly, with ongoing research focusing on improving model architectures, training algorithms, and interpretability which is shown in Fig 4.1. [Heading 4] 4.1.1 Convolution Neural Networks
[Normal] It is a type of deep learning algorithm commonly used for analyzing visual imagery. CNNs are particularly effective in tasks such as image classification, object detection, facial recognition, and image segmentation.
[Normal] Convolutional Layers: CNNs consist of multiple convolutional layers that learn to extract features from the input images. Each convolutional layer applies a set of learnable filters (also known as kernels) to the input image, performing a convolution operation. This operation captures different patterns and features present in the image, such as edges, textures, and shapes.
[Normal] Pooling Layers: After each convolutional layer, CNNs typically include pooling layers to down sample the feature maps generated by the convolutional layers. Pooling layers reduce the spatial dimensions of the feature maps while retaining the most important information. Common pooling operations include max pooling and average pooling.
[Normal] Activation Function: CNNs use activation functions (such as ReLU - Rectified Linear Unit) to introduce non-linearity into the model, allowing it to learn complex patterns and relationships in the data.
[Normal] Fully Connected Layers: Towards the end of the CNN architecture, fully connected layers are used to classify the extracted features into different categories. These layers connect every neuron in one layer to every neuron in the next layer, enabling the network to learn high-level representations of the input data and make predictions.
[Normal] Training: CNNs are trained using labeled training data through a process called backpropagation. During training, the network learns to adjust the parameters (such as filter weights and biases) to minimize the difference between its predictions and the true labels in the training data. This process is typically done using optimization algorithms like stochastic gradient descent (SGD) or its variants.

[Normal] 4.2 Attention Mechanism
[Normal] The attention mechanism is a key component in deep learning models, particularly in the field of natural language processing (NLP) and computer vision. It allows models to focus on different parts of the input data (e.g., words in a sentence, pixels in an image) with varying degrees of importance, rather than treating all parts of the input equally. This selective attention mechanism helps improve the performance of the model by enabling it to dynamically weigh the importance of different inputs during processing. Fig 4.3 shows the attention mechanism.
[Caption] Fig 4.3 Attention Mechanism
[Normal] Context and Query: At its core, the attention mechanism involves context and a query. The context represents the input data (e.g., a sequence of words in a sentence), while the query represents a specific aspect or feature of interest within the context (e.g., a particular word in the sentence).
[Normal] Attention Scores: The attention mechanism computes attention scores for each element in the context based on its relevance to the query. These attention scores quantify the importance or relevance of each element in the context with respect to the query.
[Normal] Attention Weights: The attention scores are then transformed into attention weights through a normalization process (e.g., using the SoftMax function). These attention weights represent the relative importance of each element in the context, with higher weights assigned to more relevant elements and lower weights assigned to less relevant elements.
[Normal] Weighted Sum: Finally, the attention mechanism computes a weighted sum of the elements in the context, where each element is multiplied by its corresponding attention weight. This weighted sum, often referred to as the attention context or attended representation, captures the model's focused attention on the most relevant parts of the input data with respect to the query.
[Normal] 4.2.1 Working of Attention Mechanism
[Normal] Contextual Relevance: In many machine learning tasks, especially those involving sequences like sentences or images, different parts of the input may have varying levels of relevance to the output or prediction.
[Normal] Weighted Emphasis: The attention mechanism assigns weights to different parts of the input, emphasizing elements that are more relevant to the task at hand. It effectively provides a mechanism for the model to "pay attention" to specific regions or features.
[Normal] Dynamic Attention: Importantly, attention is often dynamic and can be recalibrated for each input or step in a sequence. This means that the model can adaptively shift its focus based on the current context, allowing for a more nuanced understanding of the input data.
[Normal] Components of Attention: There are different components in the attention mechanism. The key components include the query, key, and value vectors. The query represents the current input, the key represents elements in the input to compare against, and the value represents the information to be extracted. The attention weight is computed based on the compatibility between the query and key, and the weighted sum of values is used for the final output.
[Normal] Self-Attention: Self-attention, also known as intra-attention, is a type of attention mechanism where the model attends to different positions in the input sequence. Transformer architectures, widely used in NLP tasks, leverage self-attention to capture contextual information from the entire sequence.
[Normal] The attention mechanism can be applied in various deep learning architectures, including recurrent neural networks (RNNs), convolutional neural networks (CNNs), and transformer models. In NLP tasks, such as machine translation and text summarization, attention mechanisms have been instrumental in improving the accuracy and fluency of generated outputs by allowing models to selectively attend to relevant words in the input sequence. Similarly, in computer vision tasks, attention mechanisms have been used to enhance object detection, image captioning, and visual question answering by enabling models to focus on salient regions of an image.
[Normal] 4.2.2 Advantages of Attention Mechanism
[Normal] The integration of attention mechanisms into ECG analysis models offers several advantages, including enhanced feature localization, improved interpretability, selective information processing, adaptability to variable-length sequences, and personalized diagnosis. Attention mechanisms can selectively focus on informative components of the signal while suppressing irrelevant noise, thereby improving the quality and reliability of ECG signal denoising techniques.
[Normal] Feature Localization: Attention mechanisms can localize specific features or landmarks in the ECG signal, such as the P-wave, QRS complex, and T-wave. By directing attention to these key features, attention-based models can facilitate automated ECG analysis tasks, such as heartbeat segmentation, interval measurement, and feature extraction.
[Normal] Long-term Monitoring: Attention mechanisms can be applied to long-term ECG monitoring data, such as Holter recordings or ambulatory ECGs. By dynamically allocating attention to relevant segments of the continuous ECG signal, attention-based models can assist in the detection of transient arrhythmia, silent ischemia, and other cardiac abnormalities that may occur intermittently over time.
[Normal] Personalized Risk Assessment: Attention mechanisms can be personalized to individual patients' ECG characteristics, medical history, and risk factors. By learning patient-specific patterns and preferences, attention-based models can tailor their analysis to identify subtle changes or abnormalities in the ECG signal that may indicate increased cardiovascular risk or the need for further evaluation.

[Normal] 5.2 Preprossing
[Normal] Raw ECG Signal Acquisition
[Normal] The Raw ECG Signal is the original recording obtained from sensors placed on the patient’s skin. It contains essential heart activity information but is often corrupted by various noise sources like:
[Normal] Baseline drift (slow fluctuations due to patient movement or respiration)
[Normal] Powerline interference (50/60 Hz noise from electrical sources)
[Normal] Muscle noise (high-frequency noise caused by muscle contractions)
[Normal] Fig 5.2 shows these irregularities as fluctuations between QRS complexes.
[Normal] Denoising Process To ensure accurate ECG analysis, denoising is essential. The following steps are applied:
[Normal] Baseline Drift Removal
[Normal] A Median Filter with window sizes of 200 ms and 600 ms is applied: 200 ms filter: Removes sharp peaks like the P-wave, QRS complex, and T-wave. 600 ms filter: Captures the overall trend in the signal. The extracted baseline is subtracted from the original ECG signal to correct the drift.
[Normal] Wavelet Transform for Noise Reduction
[Normal] The ECG signal processing begins with the application of the Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT), which decomposes the signal into various frequency bands. Noise suppression is achieved through high pass filtering by thresholding DWT coefficients, effectively reducing interference from sources like powerline disturbances, motion artifacts, and muscle noise. The clean, noise-free signal is then reconstructed using the Inverse DWT (IDWT). For accurate heartbeat classification, segmentation of the ECG signal is critical. The process involves detecting R-peaks, which serve as reference points for segmentation, using algorithms such as the Pan-Tompkins Algorithm. Each heartbeat is subsequently segmented within a fixed window of 700 milliseconds, ensuring the QRS complex is centrally positioned for optimal feature extraction. The segmentation boundaries are visually marked with dotted lines, aiding in the analysis and classification of heartbeats.

[Normal] 5.4 Balancing Dataset
[Normal] The MIT-BIH dataset suffers from class imbalance, where the number of samples varies significantly across different classes. This imbalance can cause machine learning models to favor the majority class, leading to poor performance when predicting minority classes. To mitigate this issue, the Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SMOTE) is employed. SMOTE addresses the imbalance by generating synthetic samples for the minority class, improving the model's ability to learn and classify underrepresented data effectively. SMOTE operates by selecting instances from the minority class and creating new synthetic samples along the lines connecting them to their nearest neighbors. This process results in a more balanced dataset, reducing bias and enhancing the model's generalization capabilities. By applying SMOTE, the classification performance for minority classes is significantly improved, ensuring a more robust and fair evaluation of the model across all classes. This technique is particularly valuable in medical applications, where accurate detection of rare but critical conditions, such as arrhythmias, is essential.

[Normal] 8.1 CONCLUSION
[Normal] This project demonstrated that ensemble models significantly enhance ECG arrhythmia classification compared to individual classifiers. Among the ensemble approaches, Random Forest (RF) with XGBoost achieved the highest accuracy of 97%, showcasing its robustness in identifying complex ECG patterns. RF with Logistic Regression (LR) and RF with K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) also performed well.
[Normal] However, the proposed CAT Network outperformed all other models, achieving an exceptional accuracy of 99% on the MIT-BIH dataset. This superior performance is attributed to its advanced feature extraction using convolutional layers and attention mechanisms, effectively capturing intricate temporal dependencies and reducing noise.
[Normal] These findings highlight the CAT Network's potential as a reliable solution for ECG arrhythmia classification, making it highly suitable for real-world medical applications. Its demonstrated accuracy and reliability mark a significant advancement toward enhancing automated diagnosis and improving patient outcomes.
[Normal] 8.2 FUTURE SCOPE
[Normal] The future scope of this work involves further enhancing the CAT Network by incorporating larger and more diverse ECG datasets to ensure better generalization across various patient populations. Additionally, exploring the integration of real-time ECG monitoring systems with the CAT Network can facilitate early diagnosis and timely medical intervention. Further research can also investigate the use of federated learning to maintain data privacy while improving model accuracy across decentralized healthcare institutions. Additionally, refining the attention mechanisms and optimizing computational efficiency can enable the deployment of the CAT Network on edge devices for continuous patient monitoring. Collaborative studies with medical professionals will further validate the model's clinical reliability, contributing to the advancement of AI-driven cardiac healthcare solutions.
