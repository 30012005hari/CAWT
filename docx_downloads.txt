analysis plays a critical role in the early detection and diagnosis of cardiovascular diseases. However, manual interpretation of ECG signals is time-consuming and requires expert cardiologists, making automated arrhythmia detection systems highly desirable. In this project, a novel deep learning architecture called the Cross-Attentive Wavelet Transformer (CAWT) is proposed for accurate ECG arrhythmia classification.The proposed CAWT model combines time-domain convolutional feature extraction, multi-resolution spectral analysis, and transformer-based attention mechanisms to effectively capture both morphological and frequency characteristics of ECG signals. The architecture consists of two parallel branches: a time-domain convolutional network that learns morphological features such as P-wave, QRS complex, and T-wave patterns, and a wavelet-inspired multi-scale convolutional branch that captures frequency-based representations of the signal. These features are fused using a bidirectional cross-attention mechanism, allowing the model to learn complex relationships between temporal and spectral information.The model was trained and evaluated using the MIT-BIH Arrhythmia dataset, where ECG signals were preprocessed by detecting R-peaks and extracting heartbeat segments of fixed length. To address the severe class imbalance present in the dataset, a combination of SMOTE oversampling, weighted batch sampling, and class-weighted focal loss was applied during training.Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed CAWT architecture achieves high classification performance, obtaining an overall accuracy of 99.00%, macro F1-score of 94.05%, and AUROC of 0.9867 across the five AAMI heartbeat categories: Normal (N), Supraventricular (S), Ventricular (V), Fusion (F), and Unknown (Q). The confusion matrix analysis shows strong sensitivity and specificity across most classes, indicating the robustness of the model in detecting different arrhythmia types.CONTENTSContentsCERTIFICATE	iiSELF DECLARATION 	iiiACKNOWLEDGEMENT	ivMission and Vision of the Institute and Department	vPROJECT OUTCOMES MAPPING WITH POS	viiiPROJECT OUTCOMES AND PO disease, also known as cardiovascular disease, refers to a group of conditions that affect the structure and function of the heart and blood vessels. It encompasses a wide range of disorders, including coronary artery disease, heart failure, arrhythmias, valvular heart disease, and congenital heart defects, among others. Heart disease is a leading cause of morbidity and mortality globally, posing a significant public health challenge.Cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) are a group of disorders that affect the heart and blood vessels, often leading to serious complications such as heart attacks, strokes, and peripheral artery disease. These conditions are among the leading causes of mortality worldwide, posing a significant public health challenge that encompass a range of disorders, including coronary artery disease, hypertension (high blood pressure), heart failure, arrhythmia (irregular heartbeats), and peripheral vascular disease. They can develop due to various factors, including genetics, lifestyle choices, and underlying medical conditions. One of the primary contributors to CVDs is atherosclerosis, a condition characterized by the buildup of plague within the arteries, restricts blood flow, and can eventually lead to complications such as heart attacks and strokes.Risk factors for cardiovascular diseases include smoking, poor diet, lack of physical activity, obesity, high blood pressure, diabetes, and high cholesterol levels. Additionally, factors such as age, gender, and family history can influence an individual's susceptibility to developing CVDs.The prevention and management of cardiovascular diseases often involve lifestyle modifications such as maintaining a healthy diet, regular exercise, avoiding tobacco use, managing stress, and controlling underlying medical conditions. Medications may also be prescribed to control risk factors such as hypertension, high cholesterol, and diabetes.Early detection and timely intervention are crucial in mitigating the impact of CVDs. Regular screenings, such as blood pressure checks and cholesterol tests, can help identify risk factors and allow for appropriate management strategies to be implemented. Overall, addressing cardiovascular diseases requires a comprehensive approach that involves both individual lifestyle changes and broader public health initiatives aimed at raising awareness, promoting healthy behaviors, and improving access to healthcare services. By prioritizing prevention and early intervention, the burden of CVDs can be significantly reduced, leading to improved health outcomes and quality of life for individuals worldwide.Types of Heart diseases:	Fig 1.1 shows various types of heart diseases, coronary artery disease (CAD): CAD is the most common type of heart disease and occurs when the coronary arteries, which supply oxygen-rich blood to the heart muscle, become narrowed or blocked by a buildup of plaque (atherosclerosis). This restricts blood flow to the heart, leading to chest pain (angina), heart attacks (myocardial infarctions), and potentially fatal complications. It is the damage or disease in the heart’s major blood vessels. The usual cause is the buildup of plaque. This causes coronary arteries to narrow, limiting blood flow to the heart. Stroke is the Damage to the brain from interruption of its blood supply.Heart Failure: Heart failure occurs when the heart is unable to pump blood effectively to meet the body's demands. It can result from various underlying conditions, such as CAD, hypertension, diabetes, or damage to the heart muscle (cardiomyopathy). Symptoms of heart failure include shortness of breath, fatigue, swelling of the legs and ankles, and difficulty performing daily activities.Arrhythmias: Arrhythmias are abnormal heart rhythms that occur when the electrical impulses that coordinate heartbeats are disrupted. This can cause the heart to beat too fast (tachycardia), too slow (bradycardia), or irregularly. Arrhythmias can lead to palpitations, dizziness, fainting, and in severe cases, cardiac arrest.Valvular Heart Disease: Valvular heart disease involves abnormalities or damage to the heart valves, which regulate blood flow within the heart chambers. This can result in valve stenosis (narrowing) or regurgitation (leakage), impairing the heart's ability to pump blood efficiently and causing symptoms such as chest pain, fatigue, and shortness of breath.Congenital Heart Defects: Congenital heart defects are structural abnormalities of the heart that are present at birth. These defects can affect the heart chambers, valves, or major blood vessels, leading to disturbances in blood flow and oxygen delivery. Depending on the severity and type of defect, individuals may experience a range of symptoms from mild to life-threatening.Importance of early detection and diagnosis of heart diseaseHeart disease remains one of the leading causes of morbidity and mortality worldwide, imposing a significant burden on healthcare systems and individuals alike. With its diverse manifestations and potentially fatal consequences, the importance of early detection and diagnosis of heart disease cannot be overstated. Timely identification of cardiac abnormalities enables healthcare providers to initiate appropriate interventions, mitigate risks, and improve patient outcomes.Early detection plays a pivotal role in preventing the progression of heart disease and reducing associated complications.Many cardiovascular conditions, including coronary artery disease, arrhythmia, and heart failure, often develop gradually over time, with subtle or asymptomatic presentations in the early stages. As a result, individuals may remain unaware of their underlying cardiac issues until they experience severe symptoms or adverse events. By implementing screening programs and diagnostic protocols that target high-risk populations or individuals with predisposing factors, healthcare providers can identify cardiac abnormalities at an early stage, facilitating prompt intervention and management.Furthermore, early diagnosis of heart disease offers opportunities for preventive strategies and lifestyle modifications that can mitigate risk factors and improve cardiovascular health. Lifestyle factors such as diet, physical activity, and smoking habits significantly influence the development and progression of heart disease. By identifying individuals at risk and providing education, counseling, and support for adopting healthier behaviors, healthcare providers can empower patients to take proactive steps towards reducing their cardiovascular risk and optimizing their overall well-being.In addition to its clinical implications, early detection of heart disease carries significant socioeconomic benefits. The economic burden associated with managing advanced stages of heart disease, including hospitalizations, surgeries, and long-term care, can be substantial. By detecting cardiac abnormalities early in the disease course and implementing preventive measures, healthcare systems can reduce healthcare expenditures, improve resource allocation, and enhance the quality-of-care delivery.The importance of early detection and diagnosis of heart disease lies not only in its potential to improve individual health outcomes but also in its broader impact on healthcare systems and society. Through proactive screening, timely intervention, and preventive strategies, we can strive towards reducing the global burden of heart disease and promoting cardiovascular health for all.1.2 ElectrocardiogramAn electrocardiogram (ECG or EKG) is a diagnostic test that records the electrical activity of the heart over a period of time. This non-invasive procedure is commonly used to evaluate the heart's rhythm and electrical conduction, helping healthcare providers assess cardiac health and detect various abnormalities.During an ECG, electrodes are placed on specific locations on the skin, typically on the chest, arms, and legs. These electrodes detect the electrical signals generated by the heart as it contracts and relaxes. The signals are then amplified and recorded by the ECG machine, producing a visual representation of the heart's electrical activity known as an electrocardiogram. The ECG waveform consists of several components, including the P wave, QRS complex, and T wave. The Fig 1.2 shows ECG components P wave, QRS complex and T wave. Each component corresponds to a specific phase of the cardiac cycle: P wave: Represents the depolarization (contraction) of the atria, or upper chambers of the heart. QRS complex: Reflects the depolarization of the ventricles, or lower chambers of the heart, as well as the contraction of the ventricles.T wave: Indicates the repolarization (relaxation) of the ventricles, preparing them for the next heartbeat.Arrhythmias: Irregular heart rhythms, such as atrial fibrillation and ventricular tachycardia.Myocardial infarction (heart attack): Characterized by specific changes in the ECG pattern, such as ST-segment elevation or Q-wave formation.Cardiac ischemia: Reduced blood flow to the heart muscle, often indicated by ST-segment depression or T-wave inversion.Cardiac hypertrophy: Enlargement of the heart chambers, resulting in changes to the ECG waveform. ECGs are valuable tools in diagnosing and monitoring various cardiovascular conditions. They are routinely used in clinical settings, including hospitals, clinics, and emergency departments. ECGs can provide valuable information quickly and efficiently, aiding healthcare providers in making timely and accurate decisions regarding patient care and treatment.Beat Classification:Beat classification, also known as heartbeat classification or cardiac rhythm classification, is a process of categorizing individual heartbeats into different classes based on their characteristics. This classification is essential in analyzing electrocardiogram (ECG) signals, which record the electrical activity of the heart over time. Beat classification plays a crucial role in various medical applications, including the detection and diagnosis of cardiac arrhythmias, monitoring patient health, and guiding treatment decisions.Beat classification involves several steps:Signal Preprocessing: ECG signals are pre-processed to remove noise, baseline wander, and artifacts that may interfere with accurate beat detection and classification.Feature Extraction: Relevant features are extracted from each heartbeat, such as amplitude, duration, morphology, and intervals (e.g., P-QRS-T intervals).Classification: Machine learning algorithms or pattern recognition techniques are applied to classify heartbeats into different categories based on extracted features. Common classification approaches include rule-based algorithms, template matching, support vector machines (SVM), artificial neural networks (ANN), and convolutional neural networks (CNN).Beat Types:Normal Beats: Normal beats, also known as sinus beats, represent the typical rhythmic contractions of the heart generated by the sinoatrial (SA) node. These beats exhibit regular morphology and timing, characterized by a consistent sequence of P waves, QRS complexes, and T waves on the electrocardiogram (ECG). Normal beats indicate a healthy sinus rhythm and are essential for maintaining efficient cardiac function.Premature Beats: Premature beats, also called ectopic beats, occur earlier than expected in the cardiac cycle and originate from ectopic foci outside the SA node. Premature beats can manifest as premature atrial contractions (PACs) or premature ventricular contractions (PVCs), depending on their site of origin. PACs arise from abnormal electrical impulses originating in the atria, resulting in premature atrial depolarization and distinctive premature P waves on the ECG. PVCs originate from the ventricles and produce premature, wide QRS complexes on the ECG. While occasional premature beats are common and may not indicate underlying pathology, frequent or sustained ectopic beats can be symptomatic and may require further evaluation.Supraventricular Arrhythmias: Supraventricular arrhythmias encompass a group of abnormal heart rhythms originating above the ventricles, typically within the atria or atrioventricular (AV) node. These arrhythmias include:Atrial Fibrillation (AF): AF is the most common sustained cardiac arrhythmia, characterized by rapid, irregular electrical activity in the atria, leading to disorganized atrial contractions. AF may result in palpitations, dizziness, fatigue, and an increased risk of stroke and heart failure.Atrial Flutter: Atrial flutter is a rhythmic, sawtooth pattern of atrial depolarization, typically occurring at a faster rate than normal sinus rhythm. Atrial flutter may cause palpitations, shortness of breath, and hemodynamic compromise.Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT): SVT refers to a rapid heart rate originating above the ventricles, often arising from reentrant circuits within the atria or AV node. SVT can cause palpitations, chest discomfort, dizziness, and syncope.Ventricular Arrhythmias: Ventricular arrhythmias arise from abnormal electrical activity originating in the ventricles, leading to irregular heart rhythms and compromised cardiac function. These arrhythmias include:Ventricular Tachycardia (VT): VT is characterized by a rapid, regular or irregular heartbeat originating from the ventricles, typically with a wide QRS complex on the ECG. VT can be sustained or nonsustained and may result in hemodynamic instability and sudden cardiac arrest.Ventricular Fibrillation (VF): VF is a life-threatening arrhythmia characterized by rapid, chaotic electrical activity in the ventricles, leading to ineffective ventricular contractions and loss of cardiac output. VF results in immediate loss of consciousness and requires immediate defibrillation to restore normal heart rhythm and prevent sudden cardiac death.Ventricular Ectopic Beats: Ventricular ectopic beats, or premature ventricular contractions (PVCs), originate from ectopic foci within the ventricles, resulting in premature depolarization and distinctive wide QRS complexes on the ECG. Frequent PVCs may indicate underlying cardiac pathology and may warrant further evaluation.    CHAPTER 2LITERATURE SURVEYCHAPTER 2LITERATURE SURVEYPAPER 1The study “Time-Series Transformer and Hybrid CNN–Transformer Model for ECG Classification with Genetic Algorithm Optimization” by Krishna et al. While these methods effectively capture local and global features, they often introduce increased computational complexity and dependence on convolution-based feature extraction. In contrast, the present work focuses on a Transformer-based architecture incorporating attention mechanisms and wavelet-inspired feature representation to efficiently model long-range dependencies in ECG signals and improve classification performance.CHAPTER 3DEEP LEARNING TECHNIQUESCHAPTER 3DEEP LEARNING TECHNIQUES3.1 Introduction to Deep Learning Deep learning is a subset of machine learning that focuses on learning hierarchical representations of data using artificial neural networks with multiple layers. Unlike traditional machine learning techniques that rely on handcrafted feature extraction, deep learning models automatically learn meaningful features directly from raw data.A deep neural network consists of multiple layers of interconnected neurons, where each layer transforms the input data into increasingly abstract representations. Lower layers typically capture simple patterns, while deeper layers capture complex structures and relationships within the data.Deep learning has demonstrated significant success across various domains such as computer vision, natural language processing, speech recognition, and biomedical signal analysis. Its ability to process large volumes of data and model complex nonlinear relationships makes it highly suitable for tasks involving high-dimensional and sequential data.In the context of biomedical signal processing, such as electrocardiogram (ECG) analysis, deep learning enables automatic feature extraction and classification of cardiac conditions without requiring manual signal engineering.3.2 Attention MechanismThe attention mechanism is a key component in deep learning models, particularly in the field of natural language processing (NLP) and computer vision. It allows models to focus on different parts of the input data (e.g., words in a sentence, pixels in an image) with varying degrees of importance, rather than treating all parts of the input equally. This selective attention mechanism helps improve the performance of the model by enabling it to dynamically weigh the importance of different inputs during processing. Fig 3.1 shows the attention mechanism.Context and Query: At its core, the attention mechanism involves context and a query. The context represents the input data (e.g., a sequence of words in a sentence), while the query represents a specific aspect or feature of interest within the context (e.g., a particular word in the sentence). Attention Scores: The attention mechanism computes attention scores for each element in the context based on its relevance to the query. These attention scores quantify the importance or relevance of each element in the context with respect to the query.Attention Weights: The attention scores are then transformed into attention weights through a normalization process (e.g., using the SoftMax function). These attention weights represent the relative importance of each element in the context, with higher weights assigned to more relevant elements and lower weights assigned to less relevant elements.Weighted Sum: Finally, the attention mechanism computes a weighted sum of the elements in the context, where each element is multiplied by its corresponding attention weight. This weighted sum, often referred to as the attention context or attended representation, captures the model's focused attention on the most relevant parts of the input data with respect to the query.3.2.1 Working of Attention Mechanism Contextual Relevance: In many machine learning tasks, especially those involving sequences like sentences or images, different parts of the input may have varying levels of relevance to the output or prediction. Weighted Emphasis: The attention mechanism assigns weights to different parts of the input, emphasizing elements that are more relevant to the task at hand. It effectively provides a mechanism for the model to "pay attention" to specific regions or features.Dynamic Attention: Importantly, attention is often dynamic and can be recalibrated for each input or step in a sequence. This means that the model can adaptively shift its focus based on the current context, allowing for a more nuanced understanding of the input data.Components of Attention: There are different components in the attention mechanism. The key components include the query, key, and value vectors. The query represents the current input, the key represents elements in the input to compare against, and the value represents the information to be extracted. The attention weight is computed based on the compatibility between the query and key, and the weighted sum of values is used for the final output.Self-Attention: Self-attention, also known as intra-attention, is a type of attention mechanism where the model attends to different positions in the input sequence. Transformer architectures, widely used in NLP tasks, leverage self-attention to capture contextual information from the entire sequence.The attention mechanism can be applied in various deep learning architectures, including recurrent neural networks (RNNs), convolutional neural networks (CNNs), and transformer models. In NLP tasks, such as machine translation and text summarization, attention mechanisms have been instrumental in improving the accuracy and fluency of generated outputs by allowing models to selectively attend to relevant words in the input sequence. Similarly, in computer vision tasks, attention mechanisms have been used to enhance object detection, image captioning, and visual question answering by enabling models to focus on salient regions of an image. 3.2.2 Advantages of Attention MechanismThe integration of attention mechanisms into ECG analysis models offers several advantages, including enhanced feature localization, improved interpretability, selective information processing, adaptability to variable-length sequences, and personalized diagnosis. These disturbances may occur due to patient movement, electrode displacement, muscle activity, or interference from nearby electrical devices. Such artifacts can significantly degrade the quality of ECG signals and may negatively affect the performance of machine learning and deep learning models used for arrhythmia classification. Therefore, an effective preprocessing stage is necessary before performing feature extraction and classification.The preprocessing pipeline implemented in this study consists of the following steps: raw ECG signal acquisition, denoising, R-peak detection, heartbeat segmentation, and signal normalization. These steps ensure that the ECG signals are clean, consistent, and suitable for further analysis.Raw ECG Signal AcquisitionThe raw ECG signal represents the original electrical activity of the heart recorded by sensors placed on the patient's skin. These signals are obtained from the MIT-BIH Arrhythmia Database, which contains annotated ECG recordings collected from multiple subjects. Each ECG record contains continuous heart activity including the P wave, QRS complex, and T wave, which together represent a complete cardiac cycle.However, raw ECG signals often contain various noise components that can interfere with accurate analysis. The major noise sources include:Baseline Drift: Baseline drift refers to slow variations in the ECG signal baseline caused by breathing, patient movement, or electrode motion. These low-frequency fluctuations shift the ECG signal up and down over time.Powerline Interference: Electrical equipment surrounding the recording environment may introduce periodic noise at 50 Hz or 60 Hz, which appears as a sinusoidal disturbance in the ECG signal.Muscle Noise: Muscle contractions generate high-frequency noise known as electromyographic (EMG) interference. This noise may distort important ECG waveform components.Denoising ProcessTo ensure accurate ECG analysis, the recorded signals must be cleaned by removing noise while preserving the important morphological characteristics of the ECG waveform. The denoising process implemented in this work consists of two main stages: baseline drift removal and wavelet-based noise reduction.Baseline Drift RemovalBaseline drift is removed using a median filtering technique. Two median filters with window sizes of 200 ms and 600 ms are applied sequentially to estimate the baseline trend of the ECG signal.The 200 ms median filter removes sharp peaks such as the P wave, QRS complex, and T wave, allowing the slow-varying baseline component to be estimated. The 600 ms median filter captures the overall baseline trend of the signal. The extracted baseline is then subtracted from the original ECG signal, producing a corrected ECG waveform with reduced baseline fluctuations.Wavelet Transform for Noise ReductionAfter baseline correction, further noise suppression is performed using the Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT). The wavelet transform decomposes the ECG signal into multiple frequency bands, allowing noise components to be isolated and removed effectively.High-frequency noise caused by muscle artifacts and electrical interference is reduced by applying thresholding to the wavelet detail coefficients. Once the noise components are suppressed, the denoised ECG signal is reconstructed using the Inverse Discrete Wavelet Transform (IDWT). This technique preserves the important morphological features of the ECG waveform while removing unwanted disturbances.R-Peak DetectionAccurate detection of the R-peaks is a crucial step for ECG signal segmentation. The R-peak corresponds to the highest amplitude point of the QRS complex and serves as a reliable reference point for identifying individual heartbeats.In this study, R-peak detection is performed using the Pan–Tompkins Algorithm, a widely used method for detecting QRS complexes in ECG signals. The algorithm consists of several signal processing steps including bandpass filtering, differentiation, squaring, and moving window integration. These operations enhance the QRS complex while suppressing noise and other waveform components.The detected R-peaks are used as reference points for segmenting the ECG signal into individual heartbeat cycles.Heartbeat SegmentationOnce the R-peaks are detected, the continuous ECG signal is divided into individual heartbeat segments. Each heartbeat is extracted within a fixed time window centered around the detected R-peak.In this work, approximately 0.25 seconds of signal before the R-peak and 0.27 seconds after the R-peak are extracted to ensure that the entire cardiac cycle, including the P wave, QRS complex, and T wave, is captured. Each segmented heartbeat is then resampled to a fixed length of 187 samples. This ensures that all heartbeat signals have a consistent input size, which is necessary for training machine learning and deep learning models.Signal NormalizationAfter segmentation, each heartbeat signal is normalized to reduce amplitude variations and improve model training stability. Normalization is performed using min–max scaling, which rescales the signal values between 0 and 1.The normalization process can be expressed as:𝑋𝑛𝑜𝑟𝑚=𝑋−𝑋𝑚𝑖𝑛𝑋𝑚𝑎𝑥−𝑋𝑚𝑖𝑛whereX represents the original ECG signal,Xmin is the minimum value of the signal, andXmax​ is the maximum value of the signal.Normalization ensures that the ECG signals maintain consistent amplitude ranges, allowing machine learning and deep learning models to learn relevant patterns more effectively.Preprocessed ECG DatasetAfter completing all preprocessing steps, the ECG dataset consists of segmented heartbeat signals with a fixed length of 187 samples. This sampling strategy assigns higher selection probabilities to minority class samples and lower probabilities to majority class samples. As a result, each training batch contains a more balanced representation of all arrhythmia classes, preventing the model from being dominated by the Normal class.Finally, class weights were incorporated into the Focal Loss function to emphasize difficult or minority class samples during training. By penalizing misclassification of rare arrhythmias more strongly than common ones, the loss function encourages the model to focus on learning discriminative features for underrepresented heartbeat types.This combined strategy of balancing the dataset at the data, batch, and loss levels improves the model’s ability to detect minority arrhythmias and contributes significantly to the high classification performance achieved by the proposed CAWT architecture.Table 4.2 Multi-Stage Dataset Balancing Strategy..TechniqueLevelPurposeSMOTEData LevelGenerates synthetic samples for minority classesWeighted Random SamplerBatch LevelEnsures balanced mini-batches during trainingClass Weights + Focal LossLoss LevelPenalizes errors on minority classes more heavily4.5 Feature SelectionIn traditional machine learning approaches, feature selection is performed manually by selecting statistical, temporal, or spectral features that best represent ECG signals. These layers apply learnable filters that slide across the input signal to detect important waveform patterns.In the Time-Domain branch, convolutional filters with kernel sizes such as 7, 5, and 3 are used to capture morphological patterns including the P-wave, QRS complex, and T-wave.In the Wavelet-Domain branch, multi-resolution convolutional filters with kernel sizes 3, 7, 15, and 31 are applied to analyze the signal at different frequency scales.5.3.2 Batch Normalization LayerBatch Normalization is used to stabilize and accelerate the training process by normalizing the activations of each layer. This reduces internal covariate shift and allows the network to converge faster.5.3.3 Activation Function (GELU)The Gaussian Error Linear Unit (GELU) activation function introduces non-linearity into the model. GELU is commonly used in transformer architectures because it provides smoother activation compared to traditional functions such as ReLU.5.3.4 Max Pooling LayerMax Pooling layers reduce the dimensionality of feature maps by selecting the maximum value within a given window. This operation helps retain the most significant features while reducing computational complexity.5.3.5 Cross-Attention LayerThe Cross-Attention mechanism enables interaction between the time-domain and wavelet-domain feature representations. It uses Query, Key, and Value matrices to identify relationships between morphological and spectral features of the ECG signal.This mechanism allows the model to emphasize relevant patterns while suppressing noise and irrelevant information.5.3.6 Rotary Positional Encoding (RoPE)Rotary Positional Encoding is used to provide positional information to the transformer model. Unlike traditional positional embeddings, RoPE encodes relative positional relationships between elements of the sequence, allowing the model to better understand temporal dependencies within ECG signals.5.3.7 DropPath RegularizationDropPath is a regularization technique that randomly drops entire network paths during training. This prevents the model from relying excessively on specific layers and improves generalization.5.3.8 Global Average Pooling LayerGlobal Average Pooling computes the average value of each feature map across the time dimension. This produces a compact feature representation and reduces the number of parameters compared to traditional fully connected layers.5.3.9 Fully Connected LayerFully connected layers combine the extracted features and perform the final classification. In this project, the feature representations from both branches are concatenated to form a unified feature vector, which is passed through dense layers to produce the final predictions.The final output layer consists of five neurons corresponding to the five arrhythmia classes defined by the AAMI standard.CHAPTER 6RESULTS AND DISCUSSIONCHAPTER 6RESULTS AND DISCUSSIONRESULTS AND DISCUSSIONThis chapter presents the experimental results obtained for ECG arrhythmia classification using the MIT-BIH Arrhythmia dataset. Initial evaluations of standalone architectures—a multi-resolution Wavelet CNN and a Lightweight Transformer—revealed complementary strengths: the CNN excelled at capturing local waveform morphology, while the Transformer effectively modeled long-range temporal dependencies and global context.Motivated by these findings, the CAWT model was designed to seamlessly fuse these temporal and spectral features using a cross-attention mechanism. Combined with robust data balancing strategies (SMOTE and class-weighted focal loss) to handle extreme class imbalance, the final architecture achieved exceptional performance with a Peak Accuracy of 99.00%, a Macro F1-Score of 94.05%, and an AUROC of 0.9867. By successfully synthesizing morphological and spectral information, the CAWT model provides a highly accurate, robust, and clinically viable solution for intelligent cardiac monitoring and computer-aided diagnosis.7.2 Future ScopeAlthough the CAWT model demonstrates strong performance, future research can explore the following extensions:Dataset & Modality Expansion: Integrating multi-lead ECG signals and diverse clinical databases (e.g., INCART) to provide richer spatial information and improve real-world generalization.Wearable Deployment: Applying lightweight optimization techniques—such as model pruning and quantization—to enable continuous, real-time monitoring on resource-constrained wearable devices.Clinical Integration & XAI: Incorporating Explainable AI (XAI) techniques, such as cross-attention visualization maps, to make predictions interpretable for cardiologists, and integrating the algorithm into comprehensive clinical decision support systems.CHAPTER 8REFERENCESCHAPTER 8REFERENCES[1] S.
